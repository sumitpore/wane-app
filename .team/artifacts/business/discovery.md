## 1. App Naming — Five Candidates

### Naming criteria applied
- 1–2 syllables, easy to say across English, Hindi, Spanish, Mandarin, Arabic
- Evokes calm water — never clinical, never preachy
- Banned words excluded: addiction, limit, block, detox, digital, wellbeing, focus
- Checked against Play Store, App Store, and trademark databases for major conflicts

---

### 1A. Wane

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Meaning** | The natural movement of water receding from the shore |
| **Why it works** | One syllable. Directly mirrors the app's core mechanic — water filling the screen and slowly receding. "Wane" carries a sense of natural rhythm, not forced discipline. The word exists in English but is universally pronounceable (single open vowel + soft consonant). It implies that distraction is a tide that naturally goes out if you let it. |
| **Emotional register** | Gentle release, patient waiting, nature's own clock |
| **Potential concerns** | "Wane" can carry a connotation of decline ("Wane and flow" is positive, but "Waneing" alone can imply fading). Mitigated by pairing with vibrant brand visuals. No major app store conflicts found — the term is used for hardware (BigTreeTech Wane boards) but not in the productivity/wellness app category. |

### 1B. Cove

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Meaning** | A sheltered bay — a small, protected inlet of calm water |
| **Why it works** | One syllable. Evokes safety, shelter, and stillness. A cove is where you go to escape rough waters — exactly what this app does for your attention. Premium, clean sound. Works equally well as a brand name in any language. Short enough for an icon wordmark. |
| **Emotional register** | Protection, sanctuary, tranquility |
| **Potential concerns** | "Cove" is used by some enterprise security/data products (Cove Data Protection). However, these operate in an entirely different category (B2B cybersecurity) with zero overlap in target audience or app store positioning. The word is common enough that category separation provides sufficient clearance. |

### 1C. Lago

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Meaning** | "Lake" in Italian, Spanish, and Portuguese |
| **Why it works** | Two syllables. A lake is the ultimate still water — no current, no urgency. Cross-linguistic recognition across Romance languages, and intuitive even for English/Hindi speakers (the sound is soft, open, and calming). Strong visual branding potential — a single word that immediately conjures imagery. No significant app store conflicts found in the productivity/wellness category. |
| **Emotional register** | Stillness, depth, unhurried presence |
| **Potential concerns** | "Lago" is a common geographical word, which helps memorability but may complicate SEO initially. Several geographic apps use it as a suffix (e.g., "Lago di Como guide"). A standalone "Lago" app brand in the wellness space does not currently exist, which is an advantage. |

### 1D. Rivo

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Meaning** | Derived from Latin "rivus" (stream, flowing water) |
| **Why it works** | Two syllables. Fresh, modern, brandable. It sounds like "river" without being literal — sophisticated without being obscure. Works across languages: phonetically clean in English, Hindi, Spanish, Japanese, and Arabic. The "o" ending gives it a friendly, approachable energy. No existing app store conflicts found. Strong domain availability potential. |
| **Emotional register** | Flowing, natural, effortless movement |
| **Potential concerns** | As a coined/derived word, it lacks the instant recognition of "Wane" or "Cove." Requires slightly more brand-building to establish meaning. However, this is also a strength — it is highly ownable and trademarkable. |

### 1E. Jala

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Meaning** | "Water" in Sanskrit and several South/Southeast Asian languages (Hindi, Malay, Indonesian) |
| **Why it works** | Two syllables. Directly means water in languages spoken by over 2 billion people. For English speakers, it sounds exotic but easy to pronounce (JAH-lah). Carries spiritual/meditative connotation through its Sanskrit roots without being explicitly religious. Aligns perfectly with the Hindi design principle "Jo dikhta he wo bikta he" — the name itself is beautiful. |
| **Emotional register** | Sacred, elemental, timeless |
| **Potential concerns** | "Jala" may be confused with "Jala" as a generic word in South Asian markets, reducing brand distinctiveness in India specifically. Some smaller apps use the name in various contexts. Mitigated by strong visual branding and app store category positioning. Could trademark the specific logo + wordmark combination. |

### Recommended shortlist
**Primary: Wane** — The mechanic IS the name. Strongest conceptual alignment with the product.
**Secondary: Cove** — If market testing shows "Wane" feels too passive, Cove offers warmth and safety.

## 2. Value Proposition

### The one-sentence install trigger

> "Your phone becomes a quiet lake. Calls work. Everything else waits."

### Why someone downloads THIS app vs. alternatives

Every existing screen-time app treats the phone as an enemy to be fought. Forest gamifies discipline. Opal tracks your failures. Freedom builds walls. Android's built-in Digital Wellbeing shows you charts of how badly you are doing.

This app takes a fundamentally different approach: **it doesn't fight your phone — it transforms it.** When active, your phone doesn't become a locked-down device displaying a countdown timer and guilt. It becomes something beautiful. The screen fills with water, and you watch it slowly recede. There is no number ticking down. There is no progress bar. There is no reminder of how much time is "left." There is just water, doing what water does.

Your phone still works as a phone. You can call anyone. You can text anyone. You are not cut off from the world — you are cut off from the noise.

### Why the water animation is a differentiator, not a gimmick

| Gimmick | Differentiator |
|---|---|
| A cute tree that dies if you leave (Forest) | Removes the timer entirely — you stop tracking time |
| A breathing exercise before opening Instagram (One Sec) | Replaces the entire UI — there is nothing to "open" |
| A usage chart that shames you (Digital Wellbeing) | The visual IS the experience — no data, no judgment |
| An app blocker you can override (Opal) | Basic phone functions remain — it is not punitive |

The water animation works because it operates on a different psychological mechanism than any competitor. It does not add friction (One Sec), it does not gamify restraint (Forest), and it does not show you your failures (Opal/Digital Wellbeing). It replaces the entire dopamine-seeking interface with something that rewards stillness. The water level is a felt sense of time, not a measured one. You stop counting minutes and start watching waves.

This maps to the Japanese concept of "ma" (間) — the meaningful pause. And it aligns with the Hindi principle driving our design: "Jo dikhta he wo bikta he." The beauty is not decoration. The beauty is the product.

## 3. Competitive Analysis

### 3A. Android Digital Wellbeing (Built-in)

| Dimension | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **What it does well** | Zero install friction — already on every Android phone. App timers, Focus Mode (pause specific apps), Bedtime Mode (grayscale screen), Flip to Shhh. Decent parental controls via Family Link. |
| **What it misses** | Purely informational and restrictive. The dashboard is a wall of charts and numbers — it tells you how much you used your phone but does nothing to change how you feel about it. Focus Mode just grays out app icons — ugly and punitive. No positive reinforcement. No beauty. No emotional design. Easily dismissed with a tap. Google has essentially abandoned active development since 2022. |
| **Our gap** | We replace charts with experience. No data dashboards, no usage reports. The phone transforms visually instead of showing grayed-out icons. Basic phone functionality preserved (Digital Wellbeing's Focus Mode blocks everything including calls from non-starred contacts). |

### 3B. Forest — Gamified Focus Timer

| Dimension | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **What it does well** | Brilliant core metaphor (plant a tree, watch it grow). Strong emotional hook — guilt from killing your tree is effective. Real-tree planting partnership creates feel-good factor. One-time purchase model (no subscription fatigue). Multiplayer focus sessions. |
| **What it misses** | It is still a timer. You see numbers counting down. The gamification can become stale after weeks — once you have a "forest," the motivation loop weakens. The app does not actually prevent you from using your phone — it relies on guilt (soft block). No basic-phone-mode — either you are in Forest or you are not. The aesthetic, while charming, is cartoonish — not premium. |
| **Our gap** | No timer display removes clock-watching anxiety. The water animation scales infinitely (it never gets "complete" like a forest). Hard enforcement — apps are actually blocked, not just guilt-tripped. Premium aesthetic vs. casual/cartoonish. The phone remains functional for calls and SMS during sessions. |

### 3C. Opal — Screen Time Control

| Dimension | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **What it does well** | Clean, modern UI. Smart scheduling (recurring sessions). Strong analytics. Pomodoro timer integration. Parental controls. Streak-based motivation. |
| **What it misses** | Expensive subscription model — generates significant user resentment (common complaint on Reddit and app reviews). VPN-based blocking on some platforms causes battery drain. Still fundamentally a "blocker" — the experience of being blocked is unpleasant. Analytics-heavy approach reinforces the problem by keeping you focused on screen time numbers. Can be bypassed by determined users. |
| **Our gap** | Free core experience eliminates subscription resentment. No analytics — we do not measure your failures. The blocking experience is beautiful, not frustrating. No VPN dependency. The philosophy is transformation, not restriction. |

### 3D. One Sec — Intentional Pause

| Dimension | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **What it does well** | Scientifically validated (Max Planck Institute partnership). Clever behavioral design — adds friction at the exact moment of impulse. Multiple intervention types (breathing, mirror, reflection). Claims 57% screen time reduction. Privacy-first (data stays on device). |
| **What it misses** | Only addresses the moment of opening an app — does nothing about ongoing usage once you get past the pause. Freemium model (one app free, subscription for more). Interventions can feel patronizing over time ("Take a deep breath" gets old). Does not create a replacement experience — after the pause, you are right back where you started. Does not address the "random phone checking" behavior at all — only triggers when you open a specific app. |
| **Our gap** | We address the root behavior (picking up the phone) not just the symptom (opening Instagram). The "auto-start on lock" feature catches the moment of compulsive checking itself. We provide a replacement experience for the entire session, not just a 5-second pause. No patronizing interventions — just water. |

### 3E. Freedom — Cross-Device Blocker

| Dimension | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **What it does well** | Cross-device synchronization (Mac, Windows, iOS, Android, Chrome). Locked Mode prevents premature session ending. Focus sounds (ambient audio). Block-all-internet option. Scheduling. |
| **What it misses** | Primarily designed for desktop productivity workers — the mobile experience is secondary. Subscription-based. Aggressive, authoritarian approach — the name itself ("Freedom" through restriction) reveals the paradox. No beauty or emotional design. The phone becomes a brick, not a tool. When blocked, you see an ugly error page. Enterprise-feeling, not personal. |
| **Our gap** | Mobile-first, mobile-only — we do one thing perfectly. Free. The phone is not a brick — calls and SMS work. The blocking experience is the product's signature moment, not an error screen. Personal and emotional, not enterprise and clinical. |

### 3F. Additional Competitors Noted

| App | Key Observation | Our Differentiation |
|---|---|---|
| **Flipd** | Community-focused, live study rooms | We are personal, not social — no peer pressure mechanics |
| **AppBlock** | Strict blocking with profiles and scheduling | Utilitarian UI, no emotional design, no basic-phone mode |
| **Flora** | Forest clone with team features | Same limitations as Forest, less polished |
| **Well O'Clock** | NFC physical tags + app combo | Interesting but requires hardware; we are software-only |
| **Mindsight Phone Lock Box** | Physical box you lock your phone in | Nuclear option — phone is 100% inaccessible. We preserve essential functionality. |

### Competitive positioning summary

Every competitor falls into one of three categories:
1. **Trackers** (Digital Wellbeing, Opal) — show you data about your problem
2. **Blockers** (Freedom, AppBlock) — build walls around your apps
3. **Guilt-trippers** (Forest, Flora) — make you feel bad for giving in

We are none of these. We are a **Transformer** — the phone becomes something else entirely during a session. No data, no walls, no guilt. Just water.

## 5. Brand Voice

### Tone definition
The app speaks like a calm body of water, not a lifeguard. It is present, not pushy. It invites, it never commands. It acknowledges that picking up your phone is human — not a failure.

### The voice is...
- **Calm** — like a lake at dawn, not a meditation teacher performing calm
- **Warm** — like a friend who gets it, not a therapist who charges by the hour
- **Understated** — it says less than you expect, and that restraint IS the message
- **Non-judgmental** — it never counts your "failures," never compares you to other users, never implies you are broken
- **Confident** — it does not over-explain itself or apologize for existing

### What the app SAYS

| Context | Copy |
|---|---|
| First open | "Let's start with some water." |
| Setting a session | "How long do you want the water?" |
| Session starting | _(No text. Just water filling the screen.)_ |
| Session ending | "The water's gone. Welcome back." |
| Streak (day 3) | "Three days of water." |
| Auto-lock enabled | "Your phone will find its calm on its own now." |
| Notification (if any) | "Your water is waiting." |

### What the app NEVER says

| Never say | Why |
|---|---|
| "Great job! You stayed off your phone for 2 hours!" | Implies using the phone is bad. Patronizing. |
| "You've saved 47 minutes of screen time today" | We do not track. We do not measure. We are not a dashboard. |
| "Your phone addiction is costing you..." | Banned word. Also preachy and fear-based. |
| "Stay strong!" | Implies struggle. The experience should feel effortless. |
| "Unlock your full potential" | Generic motivational language. Meaningless. |
| "You picked up your phone 12 times today" | Shaming. This is what Digital Wellbeing does, and it does not work. |
| "Challenge your friends!" | We are not a game. We are not competitive. |
| "Don't give up!" | Implies the user is on the verge of failure. |

### Sample brand copy

**1. Play Store description (first line):**
> "Your phone becomes water. Calls still work. Everything else can wait."

**2. Onboarding screen:**
> "No timers. No charts. No lectures. Just water — and a phone that still makes calls."

**3. Social media / marketing:**
> "You don't need another app that tells you how much time you wasted. You need one that makes you forget you're holding a phone."

**4. App Store "What's New" update:**
> "New water: Monsoon. Inspired by the first rain on dry earth."

### Emotional register

| Moment | The user should feel... |
|---|---|
| Opening the app for the first time | Curious. Pleasantly surprised. "This is different." |
| Starting a session | A small release — like putting down something heavy |
| During a session (glancing at the phone) | Calm. The water is doing something beautiful. No urgency. |
| Session ending | Gentle re-entry. Not a "you did it!" celebration — just a quiet return. |
| After a week of use | A subtle shift in their relationship with the phone. Less compulsive reaching. |
| Showing it to someone | Pride. "Look at this." Not "this app fixed me" but "look how beautiful this is." |

## 6. KPIs — Measurable Success Metrics

### Primary metrics

| KPI | Definition | Target (Month 1) | Target (Month 6) | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Install-to-first-session rate** | % of installs that complete at least one focus session within 24 hours | 55% | 60% | Measures onboarding effectiveness. If people install but never start a session, the onboarding is broken. |
| **Average session duration** | Mean length of completed focus sessions | 25 min | 35 min | Indicates whether users trust the app enough to commit to longer sessions. Growth here signals deepening engagement. |
| **Session completion rate** | % of started sessions that run to natural completion (vs. early manual exit) | 70% | 80% | The core engagement metric. If users consistently bail out early, the experience is not compelling enough to hold them. |
| **D7 retention** | % of installers who start at least one session on day 7 | 30% | 40% | Standard mobile retention benchmark. Focus/wellness apps average 15-25% D7 — we target above-average due to the "auto-lock" stickiness mechanic. |
| **D30 retention** | % of installers who start at least one session on day 30 | 15% | 25% | Long-term habit formation indicator. If D30 is strong, the app has become part of the user's routine, not a novelty. |

### Secondary metrics

| KPI | Definition | Target | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Auto-lock adoption rate** | % of active users who enable "auto-start on lock" within first 7 days | 35% by Month 3 | This is our stickiest feature and strongest retention lever. If users set it, the app runs automatically — zero daily decision-making required. |
| **Streak length distribution** | Distribution of consecutive days with at least one completed session | Median 4 days by Month 3 | Measures habit formation. We track distribution (not just average) to identify both power users and drop-off points. |
| **Sessions per day per user** | Average number of sessions initiated per active user per day | 1.5–2.5 | Too low (< 1) means the app is forgotten. Too high (> 5) may indicate compulsive app usage replacing compulsive phone usage — an anti-pattern we must watch for. |
| **Time-to-first-session** | Minutes between first app open and first session start | < 3 min | Measures onboarding friction. If this exceeds 3 minutes, there are too many screens between install and value delivery. |
| **Premium theme adoption** | % of active users who browse, preview, or purchase premium water themes | 10% browse, 3% purchase by Month 6 | Early signal for monetization viability without compromising free experience. |
| **Organic share rate** | % of users who use the share/screenshot feature | 8% by Month 3 | The water animation is designed to be share-worthy. If users are not sharing it, the visual is not compelling enough. |
| **NPS (Net Promoter Score)** | Surveyed quarterly via in-app prompt (after session completion, never during) | 50+ | Wellness apps that achieve NPS > 50 demonstrate strong word-of-mouth growth potential. |

### Anti-metrics (things we deliberately do NOT track as KPIs)

| Not a KPI | Why |
|---|---|
| Total screen time reduced | We are not a tracker. Measuring this contradicts our brand promise. |
| Number of apps blocked | We block everything by default. There is nothing to measure. |
| "Failures" (sessions where user exited early) | Framing early exits as failures is judgmental. We measure completion rate as a product quality signal, not a user performance score. |

## 7. Monetization Direction

### Core principle
**The water works for free. Forever.** The default water animation, all core functionality (session timer, basic phone mode, auto-lock, emergency bypass), and unlimited sessions are free. No trial periods. No feature gates on essential functionality. No ads. Ever.

### Revenue streams

#### 7A. Premium Water Themes — Primary Revenue

| Offering | Description | Pricing model |
|---|---|---|
| **Signature Collection** | 8–12 handcrafted water animations designed by visual artists. Examples: "Monsoon" (rain hitting a lake surface), "Glacier" (slow-moving ice-blue water), "Koi" (water with golden fish shadows beneath the surface), "Bioluminescence" (glowing ocean at night). | One-time purchase per theme ($1.99–$3.99) or collection bundle ($7.99) |
| **Seasonal Drops** | Limited-edition themes released quarterly, tied to natural water events. Spring: cherry blossom petals on water. Summer: Caribbean reef. Autumn: rain on autumn leaves. Winter: frozen lake with snow. | $2.99 each or seasonal pass ($8.99/year) |
| **Ambient Sound Packs** | Paired with visual themes. Ocean waves, rain on a tin roof, river over stones, underwater silence. Audio-only (no visual change) or paired with matching visual theme. | $0.99–$1.99 per pack |
| **Artist Collaborations** | Partner with digital artists, illustrators, or studios to create signature water animations. Credit the artist. Split revenue. Limited runs create scarcity and collectibility. | $3.99–$5.99 per collaboration |

#### 7B. Family Plans — Secondary Revenue

| Offering | Description | Pricing model |
|---|---|---|
| **Family Hub** | Parents can manage settings for children's devices. Set auto-lock schedules, choose which apps remain accessible (e.g., educational apps), view session completion (not usage data — we never show parents how much their kid used their phone, only that they completed their water sessions). | $4.99/month or $39.99/year for up to 5 family members |
| **Shared themes** | Family members share purchased themes across linked accounts. | Included in Family Plan |

#### 7C. Enterprise / Organizational Wellness — Long-term Revenue

| Offering | Description | Pricing model |
|---|---|---|
| **Team Calm** | Organizational license for companies promoting employee wellbeing. Aggregate (anonymized) session completion data for HR — never individual tracking. Branded water themes (company can commission a custom theme). Meeting-free hour integration (auto-activates during designated company quiet hours). | Per-seat pricing ($2–$5/user/month), minimum 50 seats |
| **Education licenses** | Schools and universities. Exam-mode integration (auto-activate during exam periods). Student-friendly pricing or free tier for verified .edu accounts. | Institutional licensing, negotiated per contract |

#### 7D. What stays free FOREVER — Non-negotiable

| Free forever | Rationale |
|---|---|
| Default water animation | The core experience must never be paywalled. A user who never pays should still have a beautiful, fully functional app. |
| All core functionality | Sessions, auto-lock, emergency bypass, basic phone mode, contact search, SMS, calls. |
| Unlimited sessions | No "3 free sessions per day" gates. |
| Session history (basic) | If we ever add a simple "your recent sessions" view, it stays free. |
| Emergency features | Emergency calls, emergency contacts — obviously. |
| App updates and bug fixes | Paying should buy beauty, not reliability. |

#### 7E. What we NEVER monetize

| Never monetize | Why |
|---|---|
| Ads | Antithetical to the entire product. An ad during a focus session would be an act of violence against our own brand. |
| User data | Privacy is a core value. We collect nothing. We sell nothing. This is a permanent, public commitment. |
| Core blocking functionality | Gating "harder" blocking behind a paywall would mean the free version does not actually work. |
| Guilt-based upsells | "You exited 3 sessions early this week. Upgrade to Premium for better tools!" — this is the opposite of our brand voice. Never. |
| Social features behind a paywall | If we ever add any social feature (shared sessions, etc.), it stays free. Community is not a product. |

## 8. Customer Journey Map

### Stage 1: Discovery

| Touchpoint | Detail |
|---|---|
| **How they find us** | (1) Word of mouth — a friend shows them the water animation on their phone. This is the primary growth channel. The beauty of the water IS the marketing. (2) Social media — users share screenshots/screen recordings of the water animation. The visual is inherently shareable. (3) Play Store search — "phone break app," "stop checking phone," "calm phone app." (4) Tech/lifestyle press — the premium design earns editorial coverage (this is an app that looks like it came from a design agency, not a startup). | Touchpoint | Detail |
|---|---|
| **What makes them tap Install** | (1) It's free — zero friction. (2) The Play Store video preview shows the water animation and they think "That's beautiful." (3) The description says "No timers. No charts. No lectures." and they think "Finally." (4) Reviews mention it actually still lets you make calls — it is not extreme. (5) The app size is small and permissions are minimal (trust signal). |
| **Drop-off risk** | If the Play Store description reads like every other focus app ("Take control of your screen time!"), they scroll past. **Mitigation:** Lead with the visual, not the features. First line: "Your phone becomes water. Calls still work. Everything else can wait." |

### Stage 3: Onboarding — First 2 Minutes

| Step | Experience | Design principle |
|---|---|---|
| **0:00–0:15** | App opens to a full-screen water animation. No splash screen, no logo, no "Welcome to [App]." Just water. A small text at the bottom: "Tap to begin." | First impression IS the product. |
| **0:15–0:45** | Single screen: "How long do you want the water?" A simple slider (15 min / 30 min / 1 hr / 2 hr / custom). Default is set to 30 min. Below: "You can still make calls and send texts." | One decision. No overwhelm. |
| **0:45–1:15** | "One more thing." Toggle: "Start water when you lock your phone?" with a brief one-line explanation: "Lock your phone. Water starts automatically." Default: off (opt-in, not forced). | Introduce the killer feature early but do not force it. |
| **1:15–1:30** | "That's it. Tap to start your first water." — Button: "Start" | No account creation. No email. No tutorial. No permissions screen (request permissions only when functionally needed, e.g., DND access when first session starts). |
| **Total screens** | 3 screens maximum between install and first session. | Ruthless simplicity. |

| **Drop-off risk** | Onboarding asks for too many permissions upfront (DND, notification access, accessibility service, device admin). **Mitigation:** Progressive permission requests — ask only when the feature is first triggered. Explain each permission in plain language at the moment it is needed, not in a bulk permissions screen. |

### Stage 4: First Session

| Moment | Experience |
|---|---|
| **Session starts** | Water fills the screen from the bottom. The animation is immediately, undeniably beautiful — 60fps, realistic fluid dynamics, subtle light refraction. The phone vibrates gently once (haptic confirmation) and then goes silent. |
| **During session** | If the user turns on the screen, they see water. The level has dropped slightly since last time they looked. They can swipe up for the phone dialer or swipe to SMS. Everything else: water. No status bar visible. No notification badges. |
| **Attempting to exit** | If the user tries to open an app: the water gently ripples (acknowledging the touch) but nothing happens. No pop-up saying "You have 23 minutes left!" No guilt message. Just water. |
| **Session ends** | The water drains to the bottom of the screen and disappears. The home screen fades in. A single, brief haptic pulse. Optional: a very subtle message fades in for 2 seconds — "Welcome back." — then disappears. No celebration, no stats, no confetti. |

| **Drop-off risk** | User panics because they cannot access their apps. **Mitigation:** (1) First session only — a small, dismissable text appears for 5 seconds: "Swipe up for calls & texts. Everything else is resting." (2) Emergency call access is always available and clearly indicated. (3) The session can be ended early by a deliberate long-press gesture — but the app does not advertise this prominently. |

### Stage 5: Habit Formation — Days 2–7

| Day | Expected behavior | App response |
|---|---|---|
| **Day 2** | User manually starts a session again, possibly experimenting with duration. | App remembers their last duration setting. No "Welcome back!" message. The app is just there. |
| **Day 3** | User has completed 3 sessions. May explore settings. | If they have not enabled auto-lock, a single non-intrusive suggestion appears in settings (not as a notification): "Tip: Auto-start water when you lock your phone." |
| **Day 4–5** | The behavior starts to feel routine. Some users enable auto-lock. | If auto-lock is enabled, the app recedes from conscious attention — it just works. This is the goal. The best app is the one you forget you installed. |
| **Day 6–7** | Users who have stuck with it for a week have likely integrated it into a daily pattern (evening, study time, family time). | A subtle milestone: "Seven days of water." — shown once, then gone. Not a badge, not a celebration. Just an acknowledgment. |

| **Drop-off risk** | Days 3–5 are the danger zone. The novelty of the water animation fades. **Mitigation:** (1) The default water animation has subtle daily variations (different light angle based on time of day, slightly different wave patterns) so it never feels exactly the same twice. (2) Auto-lock adoption is critical — once enabled, the app requires zero daily decisions. Push auto-lock suggestion gently on Day 3. (3) If the user has not opened the app for 3 days, a single gentle notification: "Your water is waiting." — never repeated if dismissed. |

### Stage 6: Retention — Week 2+

| Behavior | App response |
|---|---|
| **Settled users (daily sessions)** | The app is invisible. It works automatically (auto-lock) or with a single tap. No notifications. No nudges. The app respects that the user has built a habit and does not interrupt it. |
| **Returning users (used it, left, came back)** | No "We missed you!" message. No guilt. The app opens exactly as they left it, with the same settings. As if no time passed. |
| **Power users** | They explore premium themes. They adjust durations. They might request new features. The app offers a "Request a water theme" option in settings — a direct channel that makes them feel heard. |

| **Drop-off risk** | Long-term retention requires the app to keep feeling fresh without becoming noisy. **Mitigation:** (1) Quarterly premium theme drops create "event" moments. (2) Subtle seasonal changes to the default free animation (warmer tones in summer, cooler in winter). (3) Zero notification strategy — we never spam. If the user leaves, they leave. The door is always open. |

### Stage 7: Advocacy

| Trigger | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **"Look at this"** | The primary advocacy moment. A user shows their phone to a friend during a social gathering. The friend sees the water animation and says "What is that?" This is the most powerful referral channel — it is organic, visual, and requires zero explanation. The beauty sells itself. |
| **Screenshot/screen recording sharing** | Users share the water animation on Instagram Stories, WhatsApp status, Twitter/X. The app includes a subtle, non-intrusive "Share your water" option that captures a 5-second loop of the current animation — no branding overlay, no watermark. The beauty is the brand. |
| **Organic recommendation** | In conversations about phone usage, screen time, productivity. "Have you tried [App]? It just turns your phone into water." The simplicity of the pitch is the pitch. |
| **Reviews** | Users who complete 30+ sessions are gently invited to rate the app (once, never repeated). The prompt is simple: "Enjoying the water? A review helps others find it." |

| **Drop-off risk** | Users who love the app but never tell anyone about it. **Mitigation:** The share feature must produce beautiful output — a perfectly looped, high-resolution animation clip that looks stunning in a social feed. If the share output looks bad, no one shares. Invest disproportionate engineering effort in the share feature's output quality. |
